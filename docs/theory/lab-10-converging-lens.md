# Theory Reference: Converging (Convex) Lens (Lab 10)

**PHY 114 | Lab 10**

Background reading for the Converging Lens lab. Covers how a converging lens forms
images, the thin lens equation, the distinction between real and virtual images, and
the magnification produced by the lens.

---

## How a Converging Lens Works

A **converging lens** (also called a convex or positive lens) is thicker at its center
than at its edges. When parallel rays of light enter from one side, the lens refracts
them so they converge to a single point on the other side. That point is the **focal
point**, and its distance from the lens center is the **focal length** f.

A longer focal length means the lens bends light less steeply. A shorter focal length
means light converges more quickly. The focal length is determined by the curvature of
the lens surfaces and the refractive index of the lens material.

---

## The Thin Lens Equation

For a thin lens (one whose thickness is negligible compared with the other distances
in the system), the object distance d0, the image distance di, and the focal length f
are related by:

1/f = 1/d0 + 1/di

**Sign convention used in this lab:**

- Object distance d0 is always positive (object to the left of the lens).
- Image distance di is positive for a real image (image to the right of the lens) and
  negative for a virtual image (image on the same side as the object).
- Focal length f is positive for a converging lens.

---

## Real and Virtual Images

A **real image** forms when refracted rays actually converge at a point. Because light
physically meets there, the image can be projected onto a screen placed at that
location. Real images form on the opposite side of the lens from the object (di > 0)
and are always **inverted**.

A **virtual image** forms when refracted rays diverge after passing through the lens.
The rays never actually meet, but their backward extensions appear to originate from a
point on the same side of the lens as the object. A virtual image cannot be projected
onto a screen. Virtual images are always **upright**.

For a converging lens:

- When d0 > f: the image is real, inverted, and on the far side of the lens.
- When d0 = f: refracted rays emerge parallel and no image forms at any finite distance.
- When d0 < f: the image is virtual, upright, and appears on the same side as the
  object. The lens acts as a magnifying glass.

---

## Magnification

The **lateral magnification** M describes how the image size compares to the object
size. Two equivalent formulas apply:

M = -di / d0 = hi / h0

where h0 is the object height and hi is the image height.

A negative M means the image is inverted (real image). A positive M means the image is
upright (virtual image). The absolute value |M| gives the size ratio: |M| > 1 means
the image is larger than the object; |M| < 1 means it is smaller.

> **Note:** An inverted image has a negative height by sign convention: if the object
> points upward (h0 > 0) and the image is inverted, then hi < 0 and M < 0. When you
> measure hi in the simulation, record it as negative if the image is upside down
> relative to the object.
