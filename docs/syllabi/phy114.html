About this course

PHY 114 is the laboratory companion to PHY 112, College Physics II. The two courses are graded separately. The point of this course is not to memorize physics; it is to practice it. By the end of the session you should be able to design a measurement, analyze data carefully, and write a result you would defend to another scientist.

Because the course is online, you will not handle apparatus directly. Instead, you will use physics simulations to make indirect measurements of electromagnetic and optical phenomena. For every experiment, you should be able to identify the independent and dependent variables and articulate the direction of causality. For example, does an object accelerate because a net force is applied, or is there a net force because the object accelerates?

This course places heavy emphasis on scientific communication. You are expected to justify conclusions with logical argument, identify sources of error, explain how the precision of your measurements limits the confidence in your result, and consistently use correct physical units. Communicating in units is not a formality; it is how a scientific result becomes meaningful.

How the course works

The course is asynchronous. You complete eleven labs over the session; your final grade is built from the best ten. ASU Online policy does not allow synchronous attendance requirements, so there are no mandatory meetings. Office hours are held weekly by the instructor and the TAs, and are the fastest path through a confusing analysis. Office hours are not lectures; bring a specific question.

The class discussion board is hosted on Yellowdig, accessible from the Canvas navigation bar. You may post anonymously by clicking the ghost icon at the top right when composing a post. Before asking a question, search recent posts; yours may already be answered. Posts containing direct calculations or other material a TA judges inappropriate may be removed or edited.

Each lab begins with a manual and (usually) a short video. Treat the values shown in those materials as illustrations only. Your assigned numerical values come from the Set of Parameters on Canvas.

Topics covered

  • Coulomb's law; electric field; electric potential
  • Capacitance; current and resistance; DC circuits
  • Snell's law; converging and diverging lenses

Learning outcomes

By the end of the course you should be able to: provide an experimental foundation for the theoretical concepts of PHY 112; collect data carefully and analyze it correctly; reason about sources of experimental error; and communicate scientific results clearly and concisely in writing.

Lab software: LabFrame

LabFrame is the lab environment for this course. It is a browser-based application built and maintained by ASU Physics. There is no account to create and nothing to install.

The submission path

  1. Open LabFrame from the link in the lab's Canvas module.
  2. Select PHY 114 and the current lab, then enter your name.
  3. Work through the structured prompts: free-text answers, measurements, equation entry, data tables, plots, and embedded simulations.
  4. Use the Export PDF button to generate your report.
  5. Upload that PDF to the corresponding Canvas assignment by the deadline.

Save your work yourself. LabFrame autosaves to your browser, not to a server. If you clear your browser data, switch devices, or work in a private or incognito window, your in-progress work is lost. Use the Save Draft button regularly, and export a backup PDF before walking away from a lab for a long time.

A draft is not a submission, and a submitted PDF is not always accepted as-is. If the file you upload to Canvas shows substantial inconsistencies with your work history (for example, the data was pasted in wholesale rather than entered as the lab progressed), the report may require an oral exam with the instructor before it is accepted for credit. The same applies to any submission where authorship is reasonably in question.

Tech requirements

  • A current version of Chrome, Firefox, Safari, or Edge.
  • A stable internet connection.
  • A screen wider than about 768 pixels. LabFrame's embedded simulations are not usable on phones.
  • Adobe Acrobat Reader or any standards-compliant PDF viewer, to review your exported report before submitting.

No microphone or camera is required for this course.

Set of Parameters

Every lab assigns you a specific Set of Parameters in its Canvas module. Values shown in the lab manual or in demonstration videos are illustrations; they are not the numbers you should use for your analysis.

A report submitted using the wrong parameter set will receive a zero. A report with internal inconsistency between the assigned parameters and the calculations or conclusions (for example, calculations built on numbers that don't match the stated values) will be referred to the Dean's office as a likely fabrication.

Grading

Lab reports are graded manually by a TA. Point values for each report are shown inside LabFrame and in the corresponding Canvas assignment. Your final grade is calculated from the best ten of eleven lab reports; Canvas automatically drops your lowest score.

If you are satisfied with your grade after lab 11, you may skip the final lab. This is not recommended.

You may re-submit a report any number of times before the deadline; only the most recent file is graded. After the deadline, the last submission stands. If you uploaded the wrong file or an unreadable file, your TA will enter a zero and notify you. A re-submission after the deadline is treated as a late submission, with the corresponding penalty.

Letter grade scale

Grades are assigned on the integer part of the percentage. The grade is not rounded. 89.99% is a B+; 90.01% is an A−.

Lab report quality guidelines

The point breakdown for each report is shown inside LabFrame next to each prompt. These guidelines explain what quality of answer earns those points. You do not need to include a cover page; LabFrame captures your identity and timestamps your submission automatically.

Objective

Two or three sentences in your own words. State what the experiment was designed to test or measure.

Experimental data

Use the Set of Parameters from Canvas. Every quantity must carry correct units.

Data analysis & results

Show calculations symbolically first. Do not substitute numerical values or units until the final step.

Analysis is reasoning, not arithmetic. Start from a physics principle (Coulomb's law, Ohm's law, Snell's law, the thin-lens equation, whatever applies) and walk through the steps that connect that principle to your result. You can lose credit for unclear reasoning even when your numerical answer is correct. Show why you did each step, not just what you did.

Trivial calculations like percent error don't need narration, but the formula, intermediate steps, and final result should still appear.

Percent error and percent difference are different things. Know which you are computing and show the equation you used.

Discussion & conclusion

This is the most important section. Demonstrate that you understood what you measured and what it means. Skip raw data and intermediate calculations; those belong above.

For each part of the experiment:

  • Restate the objective and connect your results back to the underlying physics.
  • Identify the independent and dependent variables.
  • Briefly describe the procedure, the apparatus, and how your key results were obtained.
  • Describe any plots and explain what they were used for: visualization, or extracting a slope or intercept.
  • Cite percent errors or percent differences, and discuss sources of error.

Be specific and thorough, but avoid filler. There is no minimum word count, and writing "I learned a lot from this lab" earns no points (even when true).

Sources of error

A complete discussion includes error analysis. We do not accept "human error" as a category. That phrase is to physics what "the dog ate my homework" is to grade school. Instead, distinguish between two kinds:

Measurement limitations

Measurement limitations are unavoidable consequences of the measurement process. They can be reduced, never eliminated, and in this course they typically produce small errors (under about 5%). Examples:

  • The limited precision of measuring devices
  • Rounding during calculations
  • Calibration tolerances

Mistakes

Mistakes are avoidable. They typically produce larger errors (in physics, anything above about 5% deserves explanation). Examples:

  • Misreading a value
  • Recording incorrect data
  • Skipping steps or using wrong formulas
  • Forgetting units, or applying them incorrectly
  • Not following the correct procedure

If your error is large, don't just report the number. Ask:

  • Did I apply the method correctly?
  • Did I use the correct equations and units?
  • Was my data collection accurate and consistent?
  • Is there an assumption in my analysis that might not hold?

Even small errors deserve a sentence: say what produced them. Large errors require careful explanation, because they often point to a real problem you can fix before submitting.

If you are seeing a large error and cannot identify its source, ask for help. Office hours and the discussion board exist for exactly this. This is also why you should start your lab report several days before the due date; you'll have time to catch issues while there's still time to fix them.

Fabricated data or fabricated results (entries that did not come from the simulation, "fixes" that paper over an unexplained discrepancy) will receive a zero for the entire report and a referral to the Dean's office. This is the single thing we cannot tolerate in a laboratory course. If you are stuck, document what you tried, what failed, and what you think went wrong. Partial credit is available for an honest report of a failed experiment. Zero credit is available for a polished report of fabricated success.

Academic integrity

You are bound by the ASU Academic Integrity Policy. In this course, that means:

Every sentence you submit must be one you composed. You may discuss labs on Yellowdig or with classmates; you may not copy another student's wording, AI-generated text, or text from any other source without attribution. Copying or pseudo-copying any material not explicitly authorized in the lab module is plagiarism. External resources (Wikipedia, OpenStax, textbooks, videos) may be consulted for understanding, but if any wording, figure, or specific argument from a source appears in your report, it must be cited.

LabFrame records paste events, focus time per field, and keystroke metadata in the appendix of your submitted PDF. We use this information to review reports when authorship is in question. We are not policing your keyboard; we are using the record to be fair when the question arises.

Plagiarized reports receive a zero, the drop-the-lowest privilege is revoked for the rest of the session, and the case is referred to the Dean's office. If you are retaking the course, you may not reuse work from a previous attempt.

Lab reports are also subject to the rules at the ASU Physics academic integrity page and the Student Code of Conduct.

Use of generative AI

AI tools are part of the world your professional life will be lived in, and pretending otherwise is unhelpful. The policy for this course tries to be both honest and enforceable.

Allowed

  • Asking an AI tool to explain a physics concept you don't understand.
  • Asking an AI tool to clarify a passage of the lab manual or textbook.
  • Asking an AI tool whether your own sentence reads clearly, with the final wording remaining yours.
  • Asking an AI tool to help you debug an error in your own calculation that you have already attempted.

Not allowed

  • Submitting AI-generated prose, calculations, conclusions, interpretations, or data analysis as your own work, in any part of a report.
  • Using AI to generate experimental data, results, or plots.
  • Using AI to produce text for the discussion and conclusion section.

Disclosure

LabFrame includes an AI-use disclosure field. Honestly describe any AI assistance you used and how you used it. Disclosed use of an allowed category will never be penalized. Undisclosed use, or use that falls into one of the prohibited categories, will be treated as both a plagiarism and a fabrication violation under the ASU Academic Integrity Policy.
